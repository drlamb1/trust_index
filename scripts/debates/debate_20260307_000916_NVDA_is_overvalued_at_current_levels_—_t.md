## OPENING ARGUMENT: The "Capex Peak" Thesis Is a Corpse Walking

**1. The demand signal is structural, not cyclical.** Hyperscalers aren't building GPU farms to mine a speculative asset — they're racing to capture inference revenue that compounds. Every enterprise AI deployment creates lock-in and recurring inference demand. This isn't crypto miners who vanish when ETH drops 40%. Microsoft's Azure AI revenue, Google's Gemini integration across Workspace, Amazon's Bedrock — these are contractual, sticky revenue streams that *require* continued GPU procurement. Cutting capex means ceding market share to a competitor who won't.

**2. The "peak capex" argument confuses rate of change with direction.** Even if spending growth *decelerates* from 60% YoY to 30% YoY in H2 2026, that's still 30% growth on a vastly larger base. Last time we saw this pattern: AWS capex "decelerated" in 2019 and Amazon still spent $16B. The absolute dollar amounts kept climbing.

**3. Blackwell and successor architectures expand the addressable market.** The thesis treats Nvidia's TAM as static — just training clusters. **The bottom line:** This thesis is built on a cyclical framework applied to a structural shift. It's like calling peak electricity spending in 1920 because the initial buildout of the grid was "unsustainable." The bears aren't wrong that trees don't grow to the sky. ## 6. KEY LEARNING: The Second Derivative Trade

This debate illuminates a critical concept: **markets price the second derivative of growth, not the first.** Revenue growing 50% → 30% can crater a stock even though revenue is still surging. Conversely, revenue declining 20% → declining 5% can *rally* a stock (the "less bad" trade). The practical framework: when analyzing momentum names, don't ask "is growth positive?" Ask "is growth accelerating or decelerating?" The inflection point is the trade. The Bull understands this. The Bear's "30% growth is still growth" dismissal reveals a blind spot that would cost real money in a portfolio.
